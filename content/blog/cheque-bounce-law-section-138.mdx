A dishonoured cheque is not merely an awkward financial situation — it is a criminal offence under Indian law. Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881 (NI Act) makes the dishonour of a cheque due to insufficiency of funds or exceeding the amount arranged a punishable offence. Understanding this law is essential whether you are the person who received the bounced cheque or the one who issued it. The procedural requirements under Section 138 are strict, and missing any single step can extinguish your legal remedy entirely.

## What Constitutes a Cheque Bounce Offence

Section 138 NI Act is triggered when the following elements are present:

- A cheque is drawn on a bank account
- The cheque is presented for payment within its validity period (three months from the date of issue)
- The cheque is dishonoured by the bank — either because the account has **insufficient funds** or because the amount exceeds the **amount arranged to be paid**
- The cheque was issued in discharge of a **legally enforceable debt or liability**

This last element is important. A cheque given as a gift, as security without any underlying debt, or for an illegal transaction does not attract Section 138. The drawer must have owed a legally enforceable sum to the payee.

It is equally important to know what does not fall under Section 138: a stop-payment instruction given by the drawer does count as dishonour under the Act after judicial interpretation, but a technically defective cheque (signature mismatch, post-dated beyond validity, account closed) creates different considerations.

## The Three-Stage Legal Notice Requirement

The law under Section 138 is procedurally rigid. The payee cannot directly file a complaint in court. Three mandatory steps must be followed in sequence.

### Step 1: Presentation of the Cheque

The cheque must be presented to the bank within its validity period. After dishonour, the bank issues a **cheque return memo** stating the reason. This memo is your primary evidence.

### Step 2: Issue a Statutory Legal Notice Within 30 Days

Within **30 days** of receiving the cheque return memo, the payee must send a written notice to the drawer demanding payment of the cheque amount. The notice must:

- Be sent to the drawer's last known address
- Demand payment of the dishonoured amount
- Be sent by **registered post acknowledgment due (RPAD)** — this is the standard practice to prove service

The 30-day limitation is absolute. If you miss it, the right to file a complaint under Section 138 is lost. Even if the notice is returned unserved, courts have held that dispatch by RPAD is sufficient — the drawer cannot defeat the process by refusing to collect the notice.

### Step 3: Wait 15 Days for Payment

After the notice is served (or deemed served), the drawer has **15 days** to make the payment. If payment is made within these 15 days, no offence crystallises under Section 138.

If the drawer fails to pay within 15 days, the **cause of action** arises on the 16th day. From this point, the payee has **30 days** to file a criminal complaint before the Magistrate. This complaint window is also strict — it cannot be extended unless sufficient cause is shown and courts are not liberal about it.

## Filing the Complaint

The complaint is filed before a **Judicial First Class Magistrate (JFCM)** or a **Metropolitan Magistrate** (in cities like Hyderabad). Territorial jurisdiction lies where:

- The cheque was drawn, or
- The cheque was presented for payment (i.e., location of the payee's bank branch), or
- Where the drawer maintains the account

The Supreme Court in **Dashrath Rupsingh Rathod v. State of Maharashtra (2014)** significantly restricted jurisdiction to only the court within whose jurisdiction the bank on which the cheque was drawn is situated. However, subsequent legislative amendments via the Negotiable Instruments (Amendment) Act, 2015 restored jurisdiction to the location where the cheque is delivered for collection — which is typically the payee's bank branch.

**Documents required for filing the complaint:**

- Original dishonoured cheque
- Bank return memo
- Copies of the legal notice sent
- Postal receipt and acknowledgment card (RPAD proof)
- Affidavit and complaint on stamp paper

## The Presumption Under Section 139

One of the most powerful aspects of Section 138 is the **statutory presumption** under Section 139 of the NI Act. Once the payee proves that the cheque was issued and dishonoured, the court **presumes** that the cheque was issued in discharge of a legally enforceable debt or liability.

This reverses the burden of proof. The drawer must now prove that the cheque was not issued for a debt — for example, by showing it was issued as security or that the underlying transaction was not a debt but a conditional arrangement. This presumption, rebuttable but powerful, significantly favours the payee.

## Penalties on Conviction

Section 138 provides for a sentence of **imprisonment up to two years**, a **fine up to twice the cheque amount**, or both. In practice, courts frequently direct payment of the cheque amount plus compensation to the payee as part of the sentence — effectively using the criminal law as a debt recovery mechanism.

Courts have the power to award compensation to the complainant under Section 357 CrPC (now Section 395 BNSS) in addition to any fine imposed on the accused.

## Compounding and Settlement

The Supreme Court, in a series of judgments including **Meters and Instruments Pvt. Ltd. v. Kanchan Mehta (2017)**, has encouraged compounding of Section 138 offences at any stage — even at the appellate stage before the High Court or Supreme Court. Compounding means the parties agree to settle the dispute by payment, and the complaint is quashed.

This is, in practice, the most common resolution. Courts treat cheque bounce cases as quasi-civil matters and strongly encourage settlement. Many complaints are filed not to imprison the drawer but to coerce payment, and the criminal threat achieves this purpose.

If you are the drawer facing such a complaint, an early settlement — even a partial payment with a realistic repayment plan — is almost always the prudent approach. Contesting through trial is expensive, time-consuming, and conviction remains a real risk given the presumption under Section 139.

## Practical Advice for Payees

- **Act immediately after receiving the bank return memo.** The 30-day notice window starts from the date of receipt — do not delay in consulting a lawyer.
- **Keep proof of the underlying transaction.** Contracts, WhatsApp messages, bank transfer records showing prior payments — all of this supports your claim that the cheque was for a debt.
- **Do not accept post-dated replacement cheques without resolving the original dispute.** Each bounced cheque is a separate cause of action with its own timeline.
- **File in the correct court.** Filing in a court without jurisdiction is a technical defect that can cause dismissal.

## Practical Advice for Drawers

- **Respond to the legal notice immediately.** Do not ignore it. Even if you dispute the underlying amount, a written response asserting your position strengthens your case.
- **If the cheque was not issued for a debt**, document this clearly — board resolutions, emails, agreements showing the cheque was security or was issued conditionally.
- **Pay within the 15-day notice period** if you owe the money. It extinguishes the criminal liability entirely and avoids costly litigation.
- **Negotiate** even after a complaint is filed. Courts will record a settlement (compounding) at any stage, and the complaint will be quashed.

Section 138 is one of India's most litigated provisions, with millions of cases pending across courts. Used correctly, it is an effective enforcement tool. Used incorrectly or without procedural compliance, it fails at the threshold itself.
