A will represents a person's final expression of how they wish their assets to be distributed. But wills can be challenged — and frequently are. Whether you suspect that a family member was coerced into signing a will, or that a document has been fabricated after death, Indian law provides a clear legal route to contest it. Understanding when, who, and how is the starting point.

## When Can a Will Be Contested?

A will may be challenged at two stages:

**Before probate is granted**: If you have reason to believe a will is invalid, you can file a caveat in the probate court before the application is heard. This puts the court on notice that a dispute exists and prevents the will from being probated without your participation.

**During probate proceedings**: If a probate petition has already been filed, you can file an answer opposing the grant. The court will then treat the matter as a contested probate and require the propounder — the person relying on the will — to prove its validity through evidence and trial.

In states and territories where probate is mandatory (such as for wills of immovable property in Mumbai, Chennai, and West Bengal), the challenge must come within the probate process. In Telangana and Hyderabad, probate is not mandatory for Hindus, though it is advisable. A will can still be challenged in a suit for declaration or a partition suit even without formal probate proceedings.

## Who Can Contest a Will?

Not everyone has the standing to challenge a will. The following categories of persons can legally contest:

- **Legal heirs** who would inherit under intestate succession (i.e., if there were no will) — children, spouse, parents
- **Beneficiaries under an earlier will** who stand to lose if the later will is accepted
- **Creditors** of the deceased, in limited circumstances where the estate's administration affects their claim
- **Persons with a vested interest** in the property dealt with by the will

A stranger who has no legal or beneficial interest in the estate cannot challenge a will merely on moral grounds.

## Grounds for Contesting a Will

### Lack of Testamentary Capacity

Under the Indian Succession Act, 1925, a testator must be of **sound mind** at the time of making the will. This means they must understand the nature of making a will, the extent of their property, and who the natural objects of their bounty are. A person suffering from severe dementia, advanced Alzheimer's, or a psychotic condition at the time of executing the will lacks testamentary capacity.

Medical evidence — hospital records, prescriptions, notes from treating physicians — is crucial to establishing incapacity. Courts assess the testator's mental state specifically at the time the will was executed, not before or after.

### Undue Influence

Undue influence occurs when someone exploits a position of dominance — physical, emotional, or financial — to override the testator's free will and substitute their own wishes. This is distinct from ordinary persuasion or legitimate advice. The key question is whether the testator acted freely.

Undue influence is notoriously difficult to prove directly since it typically occurs in private. Courts infer it from circumstances: the testator's vulnerability (age, illness, dependence), the proximity of the influencing party, whether the testator had independent legal advice, and the suspicious nature of the dispositions.

### Fraud and Forgery

If the will itself is a fabricated document — bearing forged signatures or containing false representations — it can be challenged on the ground of fraud or forgery. Forensic handwriting experts and document examiners are routinely used to compare signatures and establish whether the document was executed at the time it purports to bear.

### Improper Execution

Section 63 of the Indian Succession Act prescribes the formal requirements for a valid will:

- The testator must sign or affix their mark to the will, or direct another person to sign in their presence
- The signature must be placed so as to give effect to the will as a testamentary disposition
- The will must be **attested by at least two witnesses**, each of whom must sign in the presence of the testator (though not necessarily in each other's presence)

A will that fails to meet these requirements is invalid on the face of it, regardless of the testator's intentions.

### Suspicious Circumstances

Even where the formal requirements are technically met, courts apply heightened scrutiny when suspicious circumstances surround the execution of a will. The classic indicators include: the will being prepared by or at the instance of a beneficiary, a sharp departure from natural heirs, the testator being in a weakened state, absence of independent legal advice, and unusual haste in execution.

## The Probate Process and Filing a Caveat

When a will is submitted for probate, the court issues notice to known interested parties. If you wish to oppose, you must file a **caveat** — a formal objection — before the probate is granted. The caveat identifies who you are, your interest in the estate, and the grounds on which you propose to oppose the application.

Once a caveat is entered, the court issues notice to the propounder, who must then respond. The matter proceeds as a contested probate. The original will is impounded by the court, and both sides exchange pleadings before the court sets the matter down for trial.

## Burden of Proof

A fundamental principle in will challenges is that the **propounder bears the initial burden of proof**. The person seeking to rely on the will must first prove that it was duly executed — that the testator signed it and that two witnesses attested it in the testator's presence.

Once the propounder discharges this initial burden, the onus shifts to the challenger to prove the specific ground of attack — incapacity, undue influence, fraud, or forgery. In cases with suspicious circumstances, however, the propounder must affirmatively dispel those suspicions before the court will accept the will.

## Role of Attesting Witnesses

At least one attesting witness must be called to prove the execution of the will under Section 68 of the Indian Evidence Act. If both witnesses have died or cannot be produced, secondary evidence may be used in limited circumstances. The testimony of attesting witnesses carries significant weight — their account of how the will was signed, the testator's demeanour, and whether the testator appeared to understand what they were signing can be decisive.

## Hindu Succession Act Limitations

It is important to note that Hindu law, as codified under the Hindu Succession Act, 1956, does not restrict testamentary freedom as such — a Hindu can in principle bequeath property to anyone. However, certain family members may have rights that survive despite a will: for example, a child's claim to maintenance from the estate, or the rights of a surviving spouse in certain types of joint family property. These are not grounds to set aside a will, but they can affect what actually passes under it.

## Practical Timeline Expectations

Contested probate matters in Indian courts are not resolved quickly. At the District Court level, a contested will case typically takes between three and seven years from filing to decree, depending on the volume of evidence, the number of witnesses, and the court's docket. The process involves pleadings, evidence by affidavit and cross-examination, arguments on law, and then the judgment. Appeals to the High Court can extend this timeline further.

Acting promptly is critical. File your caveat as soon as you become aware of the probate application. Engage a lawyer who has experience in probate litigation — the procedural requirements and evidentiary standards are specific, and errors at the pleading stage can undermine an otherwise strong case.

## Key Takeaways

- A will can be challenged before or during probate on grounds of incapacity, undue influence, fraud, forgery, or improper execution
- The person relying on the will bears the initial burden of proving valid execution
- Attesting witnesses, medical records, and forensic evidence are often central to the contest
- File a caveat immediately to preserve your right to oppose a probate application
- Contested will cases are lengthy — realistic timeline expectations and early legal advice are essential
